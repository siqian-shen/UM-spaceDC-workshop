Overview

Data centers in orbit: infrastructure, operations, and policy

This workshop brings together participants from industry, government, and academia to explore both research frontiers and practical challenges in building data centers and computing infrastructure in orbital environments.

As data center expansion on Earth places growing demands on existing power grids and water systems, computing in orbit is emerging as a bold complement to terrestrial infrastructure. Our goal is to identify emerging issues and possible collaborations toward building large-scale space data centers — and to define how each sector can contribute to this integrated effort, from earth-to-orbit supply chains to in-space assembly, operations, and policy.

Workshop topics

  • In-space computing architectures and data-center infrastructure
  • In-space manufacturing, assembly, and deployment
  • Launch logistics, operations, and supply chain management — including earth-to-orbit supply chains connecting launch activities to "last-mile" logistics for in-space data center assembly
  • Business models, investment, and economic viability
  • Regulatory and policy landscape
  • Environmental impact and sustainability — including terrestrial data center demands on power grids and water systems, and how the energy sector can benefit from in-space data centers
  • AI and data workloads for orbital platforms
  • Resilience, security, and fault tolerance
  • In-space autonomous systems, human-robot interaction and teaming

Traveling to Ann Arbor

By air. The closest major airport is Detroit Metropolitan Airport (DTW), about 25 miles (30–40 minutes by car) from Ann Arbor, with nonstop service from most major U.S. cities and many international destinations. Ground options from DTW include rental cars, taxis and rideshare (Uber/Lyft, typically $50–70 to Ann Arbor), and the Michigan Flyer / AirRide motorcoach, which runs frequent daily departures between DTW and downtown Ann Arbor (Blake Transit Center) at roughly $15–18 one way; advance booking is recommended.

By train. Amtrak's Wolverine line serves the Ann Arbor station (325 Depot St.) with several daily trains connecting Chicago, Kalamazoo, Battle Creek, Detroit, and Pontiac. The station is a 10–15 minute walk or short rideshare from Central Campus and downtown hotels.

By car. Ann Arbor is reached via I-94 (east–west) and US-23 (north–south), about 45 minutes from Detroit and 4 hours from Chicago. Visitor parking is available in public structures and lots; workshop-specific parking guidance will be posted with the venue details.

Getting around. Downtown Ann Arbor and Central Campus are compact and walkable. TheRide (AAATA) local buses, free U-M campus buses, and rideshare cover longer hops, including between downtown hotels and North Campus.

Getting between Central Campus and North Campus

The University of Michigan's Central Campus (downtown Ann Arbor) and North Campus are about 2 miles / 10 minutes apart by car, separated by the Huron River. The easiest connection is the free U-M campus bus system, which anyone may ride — no ID required. Blue Buses on the Commuter North and Bursley–Baits routes run every 5–10 minutes on weekdays between the Central Campus Transit Center (on North University Ave.) and North Campus stops including the Duderstadt Center and Pierpont Commons; the ride takes about 10–15 minutes. Rideshare (Uber/Lyft) between the two campuses typically costs $8–12 and takes under 10 minutes. Walking is possible but not recommended between sessions — it's a 35–45 minute uphill route via the Broadway or Fuller Road bridges. TheRide (AAATA) public buses also connect downtown with the Plymouth Road corridor near the DoubleTree.

Poster Submission

Call for student posters

Students and postdoctoral researchers are invited to present posters during the poster session, reception, and career fair, 4:00 – 5:30 PM on September 24, in the IOE Building (North Campus).

Posters may address any workshop topic: in-space computing architectures and hardware; in-space manufacturing, assembly, and deployment; launch logistics and earth-to-orbit supply chains; AI and data workloads for orbital platforms; resilience, security, and fault tolerance; autonomous systems and human-robot teaming; business models and economic viability; regulatory and policy issues; and environmental, energy, and sustainability dimensions of data centers on Earth and in space.
